Our Beginning

It Started with a Fabric Market in Accra

The first time we stood in Makola Market in Accra, surrounded by bolts of Realwax in every color imaginable, we didn't see fabric. We saw stories waiting to be told. Every pattern — the geometric borders, the vivid contrasts, the wax-worn sheen — carried generations of craft, culture, and identity.

What bothered us was this: people outside West Africa either couldn't access these fabrics at all, or they could only buy them as cheap prints stripped of their context. And getting a garment made from them — a proper bespoke piece, cut to your own measurements — was nearly impossible unless you lived in Lagos, Accra, or Abuja.

So we built AnkaraWax to change that. We connect the finest wax print fabrics — licensed, authentic, from the mills that have perfected this craft for over a century — with skilled tailors who understand these fabrics, and customers who want something made just for them.

"The best dress code is the one that says exactly who you are."

— A tailor we met in Accra, 2019

Our Mission

Democratizing African Textile Excellence

We believe every person who wants a bespoke African wax print garment should be able to get one — regardless of where they live. That's why we built the infrastructure: real fabric sourcing, skilled tailors, measurement support, and global shipping — all in one service.

We don't mass-produce. Every single piece is made to order. Your measurements, your style, your story.

The Bigger Picture

Why African Wax Print Still Matters

Wax print fabric is more than a textile. It was originally a Dutch industrial product that West Africans transformed into something entirely their own — adopting it, reinterpreting it, and ultimately claiming it as a symbol of identity, creativity, and economic self-determination.

Today, the African wax print industry employs millions across West Africa — from mill workers to market sellers to tailors. When you order a bespoke AnkaraWax piece, you're not just buying a garment. You're participating in a global cultural economy that keeps this craft alive and relevant.

Every piece we make is a small act of cultural continuity. We take that seriously.
